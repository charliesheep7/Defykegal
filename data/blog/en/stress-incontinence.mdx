## When a Cough Becomes a Problem

You sneeze, you lift something heavy, you laugh hard at a joke — and a few drops escape. If that sounds familiar, you're dealing with stress incontinence, and the first thing to know is that the "stress" has nothing to do with anxiety. It refers to *physical* pressure on your bladder.

Stress incontinence is the leaking of urine when sudden force pushes down on a bladder your pelvic floor can no longer fully seal. It's far more common in men than most realize, especially after prostate surgery, and it's one of the most treatable bladder problems there is — because the muscles responsible respond directly to training.

This guide explains what's going wrong, why it happens to men specifically, and the exact steps that put the seal back.

## What It Actually Is

Picture your urethra as a tube that needs to stay pinched closed to hold urine in. The pelvic floor muscles and the urinary sphincter do that pinching. When you cough or lift, pressure inside your abdomen spikes and pushes down on the bladder. In a healthy system, the pelvic floor reflexively tightens at that exact moment to keep the seal.

With stress incontinence, those muscles are too weak or damaged to hold the seal under pressure, so urine escapes. The [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/22262-stress-incontinence) describes mild cases as leaking a few drops while laughing, coughing, sneezing, squatting, or exercising — and the pattern is unmistakable: leaks happen at moments of physical exertion, not random urges.

This makes stress incontinence different from [urge incontinence](/blog/urge-incontinence), where the problem is a sudden, uncontrollable need to go. With stress incontinence there's no warning urge — just the pressure, then the leak.

## Why It Happens to Men

For men, stress incontinence has a leading cause: **prostate surgery**. Radical prostatectomy (removal of the prostate, usually for cancer) can disturb the urinary sphincter and pelvic floor, leaving the seal weakened afterward. This is one of the most common reasons men take up pelvic floor training.

Other contributors include:

- **Age-related muscle weakening** — like any muscle, the pelvic floor loses strength if it's never trained.
- **Chronic coughing** — from smoking or respiratory conditions, which repeatedly stresses the seal.
- **Excess weight** — extra abdominal pressure bearing down on the bladder.
- **Pelvic floor dysfunction** — poor coordination or weakness, covered in our [pelvic floor dysfunction](/blog/pelvic-floor-dysfunction) guide.

The common thread in every case is a pelvic floor that can't keep up with the pressure demands placed on it. And that's exactly what training fixes.

## How to Stop the Leaks

Pelvic floor exercises (kegels) are the evidence-based first-line treatment for stress incontinence. A randomized controlled trial published in the [Journal of Clinical Medicine](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8269168/) found that pelvic floor muscle training helped men recover continence after laparoscopic radical prostatectomy. [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/healthy-aging-and-longevity/pelvic-floor-exercises-help-for-incontinence-sexual-health-and-more) likewise lists pelvic floor exercises as a core tool for incontinence in men. Here's how to put them to work:

1. **Find the right muscles.** They're the ones you'd squeeze to stop the flow of urine midstream or to hold back gas. You should feel a lift at the base of your pelvis — not your abs, glutes, or thighs. Our [pelvic floor muscles guide](/blog/pelvic-floor-muscles) covers this step in detail.
2. **Train both strength and speed.** Do slow holds — squeeze, hold for several seconds, fully release — to build endurance. Then do quick, sharp contractions to build the fast-twitch response you need for sudden pressure.
3. **Master the knack.** This is the game-changer for daily life: just before you cough, sneeze, or lift, deliberately squeeze your pelvic floor hard and hold through the moment of pressure. Bracing ahead of the spike keeps the seal closed and stops the leak before it starts.
4. **Be consistent.** It takes about **4 to 6 weeks** of regular daily training to notice improvement, per Cleveland Clinic. The muscles only adapt if you train them steadily.

## What Progress Looks Like

Be patient and consistent, and the trajectory is genuinely good. Many men see leaks reduce significantly within six weeks and continue improving over the following months. The exercises work because they rebuild the precise capability you've lost — the muscles' ability to seal the urethra under pressure.

If your stress incontinence followed prostate surgery, training is especially worthwhile: starting pelvic floor exercises early in recovery is associated with faster return of continence. Pair the daily routine with the knack technique and you'll have both a long-term fix and an immediate tool for the moments that matter.

## Common Questions

**Is stress incontinence permanent after prostate surgery?** For most men, no. Continence usually improves over the months after surgery, and pelvic floor training speeds and strengthens that recovery.

**Can I do kegels too much?** Like any muscle, the pelvic floor needs rest between sessions. Follow a structured program rather than squeezing constantly all day, which can over-tighten the muscles.

**Will losing weight help?** Often yes — reducing excess abdominal weight lowers the downward pressure on your bladder, which eases stress leaks.

Stress incontinence is not something you have to live around. Identify the cause, start training the muscles that seal your bladder, and use the knack for the moments that catch you off guard.
