If you have ever wondered whether you are still growing — or whether that ship has sailed — you are asking a question almost every man has quietly Googled. The answer is reassuringly simple, and it clears up a lot of anxiety once you know it: your penis stops growing at the end of puberty, and for most men that means somewhere between 16 and 18.

But the more useful part of the answer is what happens after growth stops. Because "the structure is done growing" is not the same as "nothing about your size can change." Let's walk through the real timeline, then the part you still control.

## The Real Growth Timeline

Penile development follows puberty, which doctors track using the Tanner stages — a five-stage scale of physical maturity. Here is how it plays out for the penis:

- **Stages 1-2 (roughly ages 9-12):** Puberty begins. The testicles and scrotum start to enlarge first; the penis has not changed much yet.
- **Stage 3 (around ages 11-14):** Testosterone climbs and the penis begins to grow noticeably in length.
- **Stage 4 (around ages 12-16):** The fastest phase. The penis grows in both length and girth, and the scrotal skin darkens.
- **Stage 5 (around ages 14-17):** Full maturity. The genitals reach adult size and shape.

By [the numbers](https://www.healthline.com/health/mens-health/when-does-your-penis-stop-growing), most of the growth is finished by the late teens, with adult size typically reached around 16 to 17. A minority of men keep developing into their late teens or very early twenties, but by about age 21 the process is essentially complete for nearly everyone. The [Cleveland Clinic's overview of puberty](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/body/puberty) lays out these stages in more detail.

The big driver behind all of it is testosterone. The pubertal surge of this hormone is what triggers and fuels growth — which is also why conditions that disrupt puberty can affect final size, and why delayed or absent puberty is worth a doctor's evaluation rather than a wait-and-see.

## Why Men Keep Worrying After It's Over

Here is where a lot of needless worry creeps in. Once growth stops, some men notice their erections seem less impressive than they remember, and they assume something is shrinking. Usually, nothing is shrinking. What is changing is erection quality.

An erection depends on blood filling the shaft and staying trapped there under pressure. As men age — or gain weight, or stop exercising — blood flow and pelvic floor strength can decline. The result is a softer, less full erection that looks and feels smaller than the same man's erection did years earlier. The underlying penis is the same size; the hydraulics have weakened.

That distinction matters because the structure is fixed, but the hydraulics are not. This is the trainable part, and it is where most of the real-world payoff lives.

## How to Apply This: Work the Part You Control

You cannot restart growth, but you can absolutely improve how big and firm your erection presents. Two levers have genuine evidence behind them, and both are free.

**1. Train your pelvic floor.** The muscles at the base of the penis — part of your [pelvic floor](/blog/pelvic-floor-muscles) — clamp down to trap blood during an erection. Stronger muscles trap more blood at higher pressure, producing a fuller, harder erection that reaches your full natural size. To start:

1. Find the muscles: the ones you would use to stop urine mid-stream or hold back gas.
2. Do quick squeezes (1 second on, 1 off) and long holds (5-10 seconds) — about 3 sets of 10 of each, daily.
3. Breathe through every rep and fully relax between them.
4. Give it 4 to 6 weeks before judging progress.

**2. Lose the lower-belly fat.** In men carrying extra weight, part of the shaft hides beneath the suprapubic fat pad at the base. Trimming that fat exposes length that was there all along — plus it improves blood flow and testosterone, which help erection quality too.

## What Progress Looks Like

Set realistic expectations. You are not adding structural length — you are getting closer to your full natural size and firmness, and holding it more reliably.

- **Weeks 1-2:** Better awareness and control of the muscles; little visible change yet.
- **Weeks 4-6:** Noticeably firmer erections and better staying power for many men.
- **Weeks 8-12:** Fuller, more reliable erections become the new normal if training stays consistent.

## Closing

So, when does your penis stop growing? At the end of puberty, usually by 16 to 18, and essentially always by 21. That part is settled by genetics and hormones, and no product will change it. But the size and firmness of the erection you actually use is trainable at any age — and that is the lever worth pulling.
