## What Actually Controls How Long You Last

Most advice on lasting longer during sex is some version of "think about something else." Recite baseball stats, do mental math, picture your grandmother — anything to pull your head out of the moment. It rarely works, and it makes sex worse while it fails, because you can't connect with a partner while running spreadsheets in your mind.

The reason distraction falls short is that timing during sex isn't a pure willpower contest. Ejaculation is a reflex driven partly by the **pelvic floor muscles** and specific nerve pathways. That's good news: reflexes tied to muscles can be trained and influenced, which is exactly why some men can go for a long time and others can't yet. It's a skill, not a fixed trait.

Premature ejaculation is the most common male sexual concern, affecting an estimated 30 to 40 percent of men, according to [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/15627-premature-ejaculation). Almost none of them address the actual mechanism. Once you do, lasting longer stops being a hope and becomes a routine.

## Why Pelvic Floor Control Works

The muscles that fire at the moment of climax — the bulbocavernosus and ischiocavernosus — sit in your pelvic floor. They contract rhythmically during ejaculation. Learn to strengthen them and, just as importantly, relax them on demand, and you gain a real brake on a reflex that used to feel automatic.

The clinical evidence is strong. A 2014 study by Pastore and colleagues in [*Therapeutic Advances in Urology*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4003840/) put 40 men with lifelong premature ejaculation, all with a baseline of one minute or less, through 12 weeks of pelvic floor rehabilitation. **33 of 40 men (82.5 percent) regained ejaculatory control**, and average latency rose from roughly 40 seconds to 146 seconds — close to a fourfold jump. That program was supervised and combined exercises with biofeedback, so the headline numbers reflect a structured protocol, but the mechanism is the point: train the right muscles and control improves.

## A Step-by-Step Approach to Lasting Longer

Real control comes from two layers working together — building the muscle over weeks, and using in-the-moment techniques during sex. Do both.

### Layer one: build the brake

1. **Find the muscles.** The next time you pee, briefly stop the stream mid-flow. Those are your pelvic floor muscles. Use this once to locate them, then stop — don't make interrupting urination a habit.
2. **Contract, then fully relax.** Squeeze firmly for 5 seconds, then consciously release for 5 seconds. The release matters as much as the squeeze; a muscle you can't relax on command won't help you mid-act.
3. **Do 10 reps, three times a day.** This mirrors the standard protocol most clinics use.
4. **Add quick flicks.** Once slow holds feel easy, practice fast contract-release pulses. These build the rapid, reflexive control you'll actually reach for during sex.
5. **Give it 8 to 12 weeks.** That's the window where the clinical gains showed up.

### Layer two: control in the moment

The NHS recommends two proven behavioral techniques for [ejaculation problems](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/ejaculation-problems/):

- **The stop-start method.** When you feel climax approaching, stop moving completely until the urge fades, then resume. Repeat several times before letting yourself finish. This teaches you to recognize the point of no return and stay under it.
- **The squeeze technique.** As you near climax, you or your partner firmly squeeze the head of the penis for several seconds to drop arousal, then continue.

Layer in the practical adjustments too: slow your pace when arousal spikes, change positions to reset, and keep your breathing slow and steady instead of holding it. Talking to your partner about pacing isn't a mood-killer — it's what makes the techniques usable without awkward stops.

## Common Mistakes That Keep Men Stuck

Even motivated men undercut their own progress in predictable ways:

- **Only squeezing, never relaxing.** A permanently clenched pelvic floor is tense, not strong, and can make control worse. Train both directions.
- **Training for a week and quitting.** The gains in the research came from daily work over weeks. Sporadic effort gives sporadic results.
- **Holding your breath during sex.** Bracing recruits the wrong muscles and spikes tension. Keep breathing.
- **Relying on numbing creams alone.** They can reduce sensation short term, but they don't build the underlying control — and too much can make sex feel like nothing. Use them as a bridge, not the plan.

## Common Questions

**How long before I notice a difference?**
Behavioral techniques can help within a few weeks of practice. Pelvic floor strength builds more durably over roughly 8 to 12 weeks, which matches the clinical timeline. Our full guide on [how to last longer in bed naturally](/blog/how-to-last-longer-in-bed) breaks the training side down further.

**Is my problem muscle control or anxiety?**
Often both. Performance anxiety is a real driver, and having a concrete physical technique lowers it by giving you something to actually do. If anxiety dominates, pairing training with the mental side works best.

**Does this also help my erections?**
Yes — the same pelvic floor muscles help trap blood in the penis for firmer erections, which is why this training pays off on more than one front. ## The Bottom Line

Lasting longer during sex isn't about gritting your teeth or mentally checking out. It's about training the pelvic floor muscles that govern the ejaculatory reflex and using in-the-moment techniques — stop-start, the squeeze, pacing, and breathing — that keep you below the point of no return. Both are backed by clinical research, and both get better with practice.

Give the combination a couple of consistent months and most men see real, durable change. Build the control instead of fighting the moment.
