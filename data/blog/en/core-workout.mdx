## The Core Muscle You're Probably Ignoring

Ask most men what a core workout means and they'll point to their abs. But the visible six-pack is only the surface. Underneath it sits a deeper, more important system — and the muscle most men leave completely untrained is the one at the very bottom of it: the pelvic floor.

Here's why that matters. A genuine core workout doesn't just sculpt your midsection; it builds the foundation for stability, posture, and back health — and, through the pelvic floor, for bladder control and sexual performance too. Train the core without the pelvic floor and you're missing a quarter of the system.

This guide explains how the deep core actually works, why the pelvic floor belongs in your core training, and how to train both together.

## What the Deep Core Really Is

Your deep core isn't a single muscle — it's a coordinated canister of four. As physiotherapists describe it, the core is the region between your diaphragm and your pelvic floor, and the key players are:

- **The diaphragm** — the breathing muscle that forms the roof.
- **The transverse abdominis (TVA)** — the deepest abdominal muscle, wrapping around your front and sides like a corset.
- **The multifidus** — the deep stabilizing muscle running along your spine.
- **The pelvic floor** — the hammock of muscle that forms the floor of the canister.

These four work as a unit to manage the pressure inside your abdomen, stabilize your spine, and support your organs. When they coordinate well, you move with power and control. When one is weak or out of sync — usually the pelvic floor — the whole system underperforms, which is part of why pelvic floor weakness shows up in our [pelvic floor dysfunction](/blog/pelvic-floor-dysfunction) explainer.

## Why the Pelvic Floor Is the Missing Link

The pelvic floor does double duty. As part of the core, it anchors the base of the canister and helps stabilize your pelvis and spine. But the same muscles also control your bladder and play a direct role in sexual function — trapping blood in the penis for firmer erections and governing ejaculatory control. You can see how central they are in our [pelvic floor muscles](/blog/pelvic-floor-muscles) guide.

That's the payoff most men miss: when you train your core *with* the pelvic floor, you're not just building stability. You're strengthening the muscles behind bladder control and sexual performance at the same time. One system, multiple benefits.

The reverse is also true. A core routine that ignores breathing and form — think breath-held, bear-down crunches — can actually drive pressure *down* onto an untrained pelvic floor and make weakness worse over time.

## How to Train Both Together

The secret to a deep core workout is coordination, not crunches. The principle, drawn from physiotherapy-based core stabilization, is to engage the deep muscles together while keeping your breath flowing. Here's how to apply it:

1. **Master the breathing pattern first.** Breathe diaphragmatically — belly expanding on the inhale. As you inhale, your diaphragm and pelvic floor descend together; as you exhale, both lift. Feel that rhythm before adding load.
2. **Learn the connection cue.** As you exhale, gently draw your belly button toward your spine while lifting through your pelvic floor — as if zipping up a tight pair of pants. This activates the transverse abdominis and pelvic floor together. That's the foundation of every deep core exercise.
3. **Exhale on exertion.** During any core move — or any heavy lift — breathe out and lift the pelvic floor at the hardest point. Never hold your breath and bear down; that spikes pressure and strains the floor.
4. **Add stability exercises.** Dead bugs, bird dogs, and planks done with the breathing-and-lift cue train the canister as a system. Quality of engagement beats quantity of reps.
5. **Train the pelvic floor on its own, too.** Dedicated kegel work builds the strength and control that your core exercises then put to use. Our [male pelvic floor workout](/blog/male-pelvic-floor-workout) lays out a routine.

## What Progress Looks Like

A deep core that includes the pelvic floor pays off across the board: better posture, a more stable spine, lower back-pain risk, and improved athletic performance, according to deep-core training research. On the pelvic floor side, consistent training brings firmer erections and better bladder control — the landmark study by **Dorey and colleagues (2004, BJU International)** found that pelvic floor exercises restored normal erectile function in 40% of men with ED and improved it in another 35.5%.

The timeline is realistic. Core stability and the mind-muscle connection sharpen within a few weeks, and pelvic-floor-driven benefits like firmer erections and better control build over roughly three months of consistent training. The key, as always, is showing up regularly.

## Common Questions

**Should I stop doing crunches?** Not necessarily — but make them part of a bigger picture. Prioritize deep core stability work and proper breathing over endless crunches, and never bear down with a held breath.

**Can I train my core and pelvic floor on the same days?** Yes. They're part of the same system, and the breathing-and-lift cue links them naturally. Just balance hard sessions with recovery.

**How does this help in the bedroom?** The pelvic floor muscles at the base of your core are the same ones that firm erections and control ejaculation. Training them as part of your core delivers that benefit directly.
