## The Muscles Most Men Never Train

You train your arms, your chest, maybe your legs. But there is a sling of muscles at the base of your pelvis that you almost certainly ignore — and it controls two things men care about a great deal: how firm your erections are and how well you hold your bladder.

The male pelvic floor is a hammock of muscle running from the pubic bone to the tailbone. It supports the bladder and bowel, wraps around the base of the penis, and clamps down during an erection to trap blood where you want it. When it weakens — from age, prostate surgery, a desk-bound life, or simple neglect — you feel it as softer erections, dribbling after you pee, or a bladder that suddenly feels urgent.

The good news: it is a muscle, and muscles respond to training. This guide covers exactly how to find it, train it, and progress it, plus the mistakes that quietly waste your effort.

## Why Pelvic Floor Training Works

This is not folk wisdom. In a landmark randomized controlled trial by Grace Dorey and colleagues, published in [BJU International](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16104916/), pelvic floor muscle exercises restored normal erectile function in 40 percent of men with erectile dysfunction and improved it in a further 35 percent within six months.

The mechanism is simple. Two muscles in the pelvic floor — the ischiocavernosus and bulbospongiosus — squeeze the base of the penis during an erection, raising internal pressure and keeping blood locked in. Train them and you get firmer, longer-lasting erections. The same muscles govern the urinary sphincter, which is why the training also sharpens bladder control. For more on what these muscles do, see our explainer on the [pelvic floor muscles](/blog/pelvic-floor-muscles).

## Step-by-Step: How to Train the Male Pelvic Floor

### Step 1 — Locate the muscles

The fastest way to find them: the next time you urinate, stop the stream midway. The muscles that halt the flow are your pelvic floor. Alternatively, imagine tightening to stop yourself passing gas — you should feel a lift around the anus and at the base of the penis.

Use the stop-the-urine trick only to *identify* the muscles. Do not make it a habit, since regularly interrupting urination can irritate the bladder. [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22211-kegel-exercises-for-men) and [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/step-by-step-guide-to-performing-kegel-exercises) both describe these same locating methods.

### Step 2 — Master the slow hold

Once you have found the muscles:

1. Squeeze and lift the pelvic floor.
2. Hold the contraction for three seconds to start, building toward 10 seconds.
3. Fully relax for the same length of time — the release matters as much as the squeeze.
4. Repeat for 10 slow holds.

Breathe normally throughout. Do not hold your breath, clench your buttocks, or squeeze your thighs together. Isolating the pelvic floor is the whole point.

### Step 3 — Add fast pulses

After your slow holds, do 10 quick contractions: squeeze hard, release immediately, no hold. These fast-twitch reps train the reflex that clamps down suddenly — the one that stops a leak when you cough or sneeze, and that boosts firmness on demand.

### Step 4 — Build the daily total

Aim for 10 slow holds plus 10 fast pulses, three times a day, in line with the [NIDDK](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/urologic-diseases/kegel-exercises) guidance on kegel training. That is roughly five minutes total, spread across the day. Anchor each set to something you already do — morning coffee, your commute, brushing your teeth at night.

## Building the Habit and Progressing

The hardest part of pelvic floor training is not the exercise — it is remembering to do it every day and knowing when to make it harder. Muscles adapt, so a routine that challenged you in week one becomes maintenance by week five.

Progress by extending the hold (three seconds becomes ten), adding reps (10 becomes 15), and shortening the rest between contractions. Guided programs handle this progression for you, which is where a structured app helps most. Consistency beats intensity here. Five focused minutes a day for eight weeks will do far more than a punishing 30-minute session you do twice and abandon.

## Common Mistakes That Waste Your Effort

- **Bearing down instead of lifting.** If you feel pressure pushing outward or downward, you are doing the opposite of a kegel. The motion is a squeeze-and-lift, inward and up.
- **Recruiting the wrong muscles.** Clenched glutes, tight abs, or held breath mean the pelvic floor is barely working. Keep everything else relaxed.
- **Skipping the release.** A muscle that never fully relaxes gets tight, not strong. Give every contraction an equal rest — this also prevents an overtight, or hypertonic, pelvic floor.
- **Quitting too early.** Six weeks is the typical window for noticeable change. Most men who "tried kegels and they did not work" stopped at week two.

If you suspect your floor is too tight rather than too weak, our guide on [how to relax pelvic floor muscles](/blog/how-to-relax-pelvic-floor-muscles) covers the other side of the coin.

## What Progress Looks Like

Expect the first signs — better bladder control, firmer erections, less post-urination dribble — around the four-to-six-week mark, with steady gains continuing past three months. That timeline matches the pelvic floor research and the experience of most men who stick with daily training.

Train the male pelvic floor like any other muscle: correct form, honest consistency, gradual progression. The payoff is control over the outcomes that matter most.
