Ask most men to name a pelvic exercise and they will say "kegels," if they can name one at all. Kegels are the headline act, and they earn it, but they are not the whole show. A pelvic floor that only knows how to clench is only half-trained. The moves that surround kegels, the ones that teach the muscles to relax, coordinate, and stay well-supplied with blood, are what turn a strong pelvic floor into a functional one.

Here are the pelvic exercises for men that actually matter, why each one earns its place, and how to combine them into a routine that improves erections, bladder control, and comfort.

## Why Pelvic Exercises Work

The pelvic floor is a sling of muscle at the base of the pelvis. It clamps the veins that trap blood in the penis during an erection, powers ejaculation, and backs up your bladder and bowel control. Like any muscle group, it responds to training, and the research backs it: a landmark trial on [pelvic floor exercises for erectile dysfunction](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16104916/) found structured training restored normal function in 40% of men and improved it in another 35%.

But strength is only one quality. A truly functional pelvic floor can also relax fully and coordinate with your breathing and core. That is why the best routines go [beyond kegels](https://www.inspirahealthnetwork.org/news/healthy-living/beyond-kegels-5-alternative-exercises-stronger-pelvic-floor) to train the full range. Start by getting familiar with the [pelvic floor muscles](/blog/pelvic-floor-muscles) so you know what each exercise is targeting.

## The Five Core Pelvic Exercises

**1. Kegels (the contraction).** Squeeze and lift the muscles you would use to stop urine midstream, hold for a few seconds, then fully release. Build from 3-second to 10-second holds over several weeks. This is the foundation. Harvard Health's [step-by-step kegel guide](https://www.health.harvard.edu/step-by-step-guide-to-performing-kegel-exercises) covers the technique in detail, and our own guide to [kegels for men](/blog/kegels-for-men) walks through it too.

**2. Reverse kegels (the release).** The opposite skill: gently relax and lengthen the pelvic floor, as if softly letting go to pass gas. This trains the controlled relaxation most men have never practised, and it is essential if your floor runs tight. If pain or tension is an issue, our guide to [how to relax pelvic floor muscles](/blog/how-to-relax-pelvic-floor-muscles) goes deeper.

**3. Glute bridges.** Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat. Engage your core and pelvic floor, lift your hips into a straight line from shoulders to knees, hold briefly, and lower. Do 10 to 15 reps. Bridges link the pelvic floor to the glutes and hamstrings it works alongside.

**4. Bodyweight squats.** Deep, controlled squats keep the pelvic nerves healthy through movement and circulation, and they train the pelvic floor to engage under load the way it does in daily life. Coordinate each rep with relaxed breathing rather than bracing.

**5. Diaphragmatic breathing.** Your diaphragm and pelvic floor move as a pair, contracting and releasing opposite each other with every breath. Slow belly breathing gently mobilises the pelvic floor and switches on the relaxation response. Hinge Health's overview of [breathing for pelvic health](https://www.hingehealth.com/resources/articles/diaphragmatic-breathing-pelvic-health/) explains the mechanism well. This is the single best exercise for men whose floor is stuck in tension.

## Building the Routine

You do not need to do all five every day. A simple, effective structure looks like this:

1. **Daily:** A short kegel session (slow holds plus quick pulses) and a few reverse kegels to balance contraction with release.
2. **Three times a week:** Add bridges and squats for supporting strength and circulation.
3. **Anytime, especially when tense:** Two or three minutes of diaphragmatic breathing.

The hardest part is not the exercises, it is doing them consistently. Anchoring the daily work to an existing habit helps, and so does having something that counts the reps and paces the holds for you. ## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Only ever contracting.** Skipping the release work leaves you with a tight, poorly coordinated floor. Always pair kegels with reverse kegels and breathing.
- **Holding your breath.** Bracing recruits the wrong muscles. Breathe normally, or better, coordinate the movement with your breath.
- **Recruiting abs, glutes, and thighs during kegels.** If those fire, isolate harder and use less force. The pelvic floor should do the work.
- **Going too hard on a tight floor.** If you have pelvic pain, prioritise relaxation and breathing before adding heavy strengthening. Piling strength onto a [tight, hypertonic floor](/blog/hypertonic-pelvic-floor) makes symptoms worse.

## What Progress Looks Like

Give the routine four to six weeks before judging it. Early wins are usually better bladder control and less post-urination dribble. Firmer, longer-lasting erections and better ejaculatory control build over the following couple of months, matching the timelines in the clinical studies. Real strength and coordination gains take three to six months of consistent work, just like any other training.

## Closing

Pelvic exercises for men are more than kegels. Kegels build the strength, reverse kegels and breathing build the release, and bridges and squats build the supporting coordination and circulation. Train all of it, consistently, and you get what matters most: a pelvic floor that can both tighten and let go on command, the real foundation of firmer erections, reliable bladder control, and a body that works the way it should.
