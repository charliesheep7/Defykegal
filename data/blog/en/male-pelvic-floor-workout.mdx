Men train their biceps, chest, quads, and core. Almost none of them include the pelvic floor — a muscle group with direct, measurable effects on erection quality, ejaculatory control, and bladder function. It takes less than 5 minutes a day and requires no equipment.

This guide is the workout: the actual exercises, the programming, and the evidence behind it.

## Why a Dedicated Pelvic Floor Workout

The pelvic floor is a set of muscles sitting at the base of the pelvis, spanning from the pubic bone to the tailbone. In men, the key muscles include the ischiocavernosus, bulbospongiosus, pubococcygeus (PC muscle), and external urethral sphincter — a group that collectively governs three functions:

- **Erections**: The ischiocavernosus and bulbospongiosus compress veins at the penile base, trapping blood and maintaining rigidity
- **Ejaculatory control**: The bulbospongiosus and PC muscle regulate the timing and rhythm of the ejaculatory reflex
- **Bladder control**: The external urethral sphincter and pelvic floor muscles keep the urethra closed until you decide to urinate

A complete explanation of the anatomy is in our guide on [pelvic floor muscles](/blog/pelvic-floor-muscles). The short version: these muscles weaken from the same causes as every other muscle — disuse, age, sedentary living — and they respond to progressive training in the same way.

The clinical evidence is specific. A landmark study by Dorey et al. (2004), published in [*BJU International*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15049987/), put men with erectile dysfunction through a 3-month pelvic floor training protocol. Result: **40% fully restored normal erectile function, and 35.5% showed significant improvement** — a combined 75.5% meaningful response rate from targeted muscle training alone. A [2019 systematic review in *Physiotherapy*](https://www.healthline.com/health/kegel-exercises-for-men) confirmed benefits for both ED and premature ejaculation. The Van Kampen et al. trial, published in the *Lancet*, established pelvic floor training as the gold standard for post-prostatectomy urinary incontinence recovery.

## The Male Pelvic Floor Workout

### Step 1: Find the Muscles

Before any training can work, you need to be activating the right muscles. Two methods:

**Method A (the classic)**: The next time you urinate, try to stop the flow mid-stream. The muscles you engage to do this are your pelvic floor. Note the sensation — the inward lift and squeeze, not a downward push. Do not use this method regularly during actual urination; use it once or twice to identify the feeling, then work from memory.

**Method B (without urinating)**: Sit or lie down. Imagine you are simultaneously trying to stop urinating and stop passing gas. You should feel a gentle lift and inward tightening deep inside your pelvis. Your buttocks, thighs, and abdominal muscles should remain relaxed — if they tighten, you are recruiting the wrong muscles.

Common mistake to avoid: **bearing down instead of lifting**. Pushing outward is the opposite of a pelvic floor contraction and will not produce training benefits. The movement should feel inward and upward.

### Exercise 1: Slow Contractions (Strength Builder)

Slow, sustained contractions build the endurance and maximum strength of the Type I (slow-twitch) muscle fibers in your pelvic floor. These fibers maintain baseline tone and are the primary muscles keeping you continent and supporting erection maintenance.

**Protocol:**
1. Contract the pelvic floor slowly, building to maximum effort over 2 seconds
2. Hold the contraction for 5-10 seconds (start with 5 seconds in week 1, increase to 10 by week 6)
3. Release slowly and completely over 2 seconds
4. Rest for 5-10 seconds — full relaxation is essential
5. Repeat 10-15 times per set
6. Do 3 sets per day

**Positions**: You can train lying on your back with knees bent, sitting upright, or standing. Different positions change the demand slightly. Beginners often find lying down easiest because gravity does not add to the challenge. Progress to seated and standing as you build control.

### Exercise 2: Quick Contractions (Power Builder)

Quick, sharp contractions train the Type II (fast-twitch) muscle fibers — the ones responsible for rapid, forceful responses. These are critical for ejaculatory control (the ability to voluntarily delay) and for the sharp reflex contractions of the ejaculation itself. They also contribute to the quick urethral closure that prevents urgency leaks.

**Protocol:**
1. Contract the pelvic floor as firmly and quickly as possible
2. Hold for 1 second
3. Release fully for 1 second
4. Repeat 10-15 times per set
5. Do 2 sets per day (fewer than slow contractions — these are more fatiguing)

**Tip**: Think of these as a "flick" rather than a sustained squeeze. Speed and full release matter more than the hold duration.

### Exercise 3: Pelvic Floor Bridge

The glute bridge with a pelvic floor contraction integrates the pelvic floor with hip and core musculature, which is closer to how these muscles function during sex and exercise.

**Protocol:**
1. Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor, hip-width apart
2. Contract your pelvic floor
3. Slowly raise your hips off the floor until your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders
4. Hold at the top for 3-5 seconds while maintaining the pelvic floor contraction
5. Lower slowly, releasing the pelvic floor as you return to the floor
6. Repeat 10-12 times
7. Do 2-3 sets per day

The bridge position engages the gluteus maximus and hamstrings, which attach to the pelvis and assist in supporting pelvic floor function under load. This is particularly useful for men who want to connect their pelvic floor training to athletic performance or functional strength.

### Weekly Schedule

| Day | Slow Contractions | Quick Contractions | Bridge |
|-----|------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Mon | 3 sets × 10 reps | 2 sets × 10 reps | 2 sets × 10 |
| Tue | 3 sets × 10 reps | 2 sets × 10 reps | — |
| Wed | 3 sets × 12 reps | 2 sets × 12 reps | 2 sets × 12 |
| Thu | 3 sets × 12 reps | 2 sets × 12 reps | — |
| Fri | 3 sets × 12 reps | 2 sets × 12 reps | 2 sets × 12 |
| Sat | 3 sets × 10 reps | 2 sets × 10 reps | — |
| Sun | Rest (or light routine) | — | — |

Adjust based on your schedule — daily is the goal, every other day the minimum. Unlike heavy resistance training, the pelvic floor recovers quickly enough for daily training.

### Progression

**Weeks 1-2**: Focus on muscle isolation — are you contracting the pelvic floor without recruiting glutes, abs, or thighs? Hold 5 seconds on slow contractions.

**Weeks 3-4**: Increase hold duration to 8 seconds on slow contractions. Add the bridge exercise.

**Weeks 5-6**: Increase hold duration to 10 seconds. Add quick contraction sets.

**Weeks 7-12**: Maintain the full program. Add functional kegels during other exercise (contracting during the exertion phase of squats, pushups, or running strides).

**After 12 weeks**: Most men have reached their main adaptation and shift to a maintenance program — 1-2 daily sets instead of 3, sustained indefinitely.

## Common Mistakes in Male Pelvic Floor Training

**Skipping the release**: Many men contract correctly but do not fully relax between reps. Full release is where muscle recovery and length are maintained. Incomplete release leads to a chronically tight pelvic floor — which causes pain and dysfunction, not strength.

**Holding your breath**: Breath-holding increases intra-abdominal pressure and pushes down on the pelvic floor, working against your contraction. Breathe normally throughout every exercise.

**Straining instead of lifting**: If you feel yourself pushing outward or bearing down, stop and refocus. The movement is inward and upward, like an elevator rising inside your pelvis.

**Inconsistent training**: The pelvic floor adapts slowly. Missing several days per week produces much slower results than daily consistency. If consistency is the challenge, a structured program with reminders is worth it.

**Training through pain**: Mild fatigue is normal; pain is not. If training produces pelvic pain, aching, or pressure, stop and read our guide on [pelvic floor dysfunction](/blog/pelvic-floor-dysfunction) — an overly tight (hypertonic) pelvic floor needs relaxation techniques, not more squeezing.

## What Progress Looks Like

**Weeks 1-2**: Building mind-muscle connection. Training may feel awkward or fatiguing quickly — both are normal early signs that the muscles are being activated.

**Weeks 3-6**: Coordination improves. Bladder control often shows early improvement — reduced urgency and less post-urination dribble. Some men begin noticing improved erection firmness.

**Weeks 7-12**: The main strength adaptation period. Erection quality improvements become more consistent. Ejaculatory control improves as the fast-twitch component of training develops. Bladder control is usually significantly better by this point.

**Ongoing**: Maintenance training preserves the gains. The pelvic floor, like any muscle, detains gradually without use — a minimal daily habit of 1-2 sets sustains the improvements indefinitely.

The Dorey study measured outcomes at 3 months. That is the realistic timeline for the full training effect. Stay consistent for 12 weeks before evaluating.

## Common Questions

**Can I do this at the gym?**

Yes — discreetly. Slow and quick kegel contractions are invisible from the outside and can be done sitting, standing, or during rests between sets. Many men find it natural to add a few sets during cooldown stretching or while changing.

**What if I do not feel anything for the first week or two?**

Developing proprioceptive awareness of deep pelvic muscles takes time. Try training in different positions (lying down first), and use the mid-stream stop test once or twice to confirm you are activating the right area. If isolation remains difficult after two weeks, a single session with a [pelvic floor physical therapist](/blog/pelvic-floor-physical-therapy) can provide hands-on correction.

**Does this work alongside other ED treatment?**

Yes, and the combination is more effective than either approach alone. Research on combining pelvic floor training with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) suggests superior outcomes because each addresses a different mechanism: medication improves arterial inflow, and a strong pelvic floor improves venous trapping. Our guide on [how to last longer in bed](/blog/how-to-last-longer-in-bed) covers the ejaculatory control side in depth.

**Is there a maximum age for this to work?**

No. Pelvic floor muscles respond to training at any age. Older men may see slower initial progress and may need to remain on a maintenance program longer, but the physiological adaptation process works across the lifespan.

## Start Wherever You Are

You do not need gym equipment, a trainer, or a lot of time. The male pelvic floor workout is a daily 3-5 minute investment in the muscle group with the most direct effect on erection quality, ejaculatory control, and bladder function.

The protocol above is where to start. Consistency over 12 weeks is where the results come from.
