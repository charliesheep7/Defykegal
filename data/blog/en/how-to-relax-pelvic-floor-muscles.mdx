Almost every article about the male pelvic floor tells you to squeeze. Kegels, contractions, "lift and hold." For a lot of men that advice is exactly right. But for a meaningful group of men — the ones with pelvic pain, ache after sitting, trouble emptying the bladder, or discomfort after ejaculation — squeezing is the last thing they need. Their pelvic floor is already too tight, and adding more contractions makes it worse.

If that sounds like you, the skill to learn is the opposite one: how to relax pelvic floor muscles on command. This guide covers how to do it, why it works, and how to build it into a daily habit.

## Why a Tight Pelvic Floor Needs Relaxation, Not Strength

The pelvic floor is a group of muscles slung across the base of the pelvis. Like any muscle, it can be too weak — or too tight. A chronically tight, overactive pelvic floor is called **hypertonic**, and it behaves like a bicep you can never fully unclench: sore, fatigued, and unable to work through its full range.

In men, a hypertonic pelvic floor is linked to pelvic pain, urinary hesitancy, and sexual discomfort. It also overlaps heavily with chronic prostatitis and chronic pelvic pain syndrome — a condition the [National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/urologic-diseases/prostate-problems/prostatitis-inflammation-prostate) describes as often involving pelvic muscle tension rather than infection, which is why relaxation-based therapy helps. The mechanism is straightforward. Muscles that never fully relax lose their ability to contract and release with normal timing, which disrupts urination, erection, and ejaculation. Our deeper explainer on the [hypertonic pelvic floor](/blog/hypertonic-pelvic-floor) covers the full symptom picture.

The evidence for a relaxation-first approach is real. A 2022 systematic review by van Reijn-Baggen et al. in *Sexual Medicine Reviews*, indexed on [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34127429/), examined pelvic floor physical therapy for pelvic floor hypertonicity and found that three of four randomized controlled trials showed positive effects compared with controls, and prospective studies improved across every outcome measured. Pelvic floor physical therapy is considered a first-line intervention for an overactive pelvic floor — and its core tools are the ones below: diaphragmatic breathing, stretches, and reverse kegels.

The key insight: **for a tight pelvic floor, the goal of training is release, not effort.**

## How to Relax Your Pelvic Floor Muscles: The Step-by-Step Routine

### Step 1: Learn Diaphragmatic Breathing

Your diaphragm and pelvic floor are a coordinated pair. When you inhale into your belly, the diaphragm drops and the pelvic floor descends and softens. This is the single most reliable way to relax the pelvic floor without any conscious muscle work.

1. Lie on your back with knees bent, or sit comfortably
2. Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly
3. Inhale slowly through your nose for 4 seconds, directing the breath so your belly rises and your chest stays still
4. Feel the base of your pelvis gently drop and widen as you inhale
5. Exhale slowly through your mouth for 6 seconds, letting everything soften — do not push or squeeze
6. Repeat for 5 minutes

The exhale being longer than the inhale nudges your nervous system toward a relaxed state, which helps the muscles let go.

### Step 2: Practice the Reverse Kegel

A reverse kegel is the deliberate act of lengthening the pelvic floor instead of lifting it. If a standard kegel is an elevator going up, a reverse kegel is the same elevator gently descending to the ground floor.

1. Start with a slow diaphragmatic inhale
2. As you breathe in, imagine gently pushing your pelvic floor down and out — the same subtle sensation as beginning to urinate or pass gas, but without any straining or bearing down hard
3. Let the muscles bulge slightly and lengthen for 5-10 seconds
4. Release the effort and return to a neutral resting state — do not contract
5. Rest for 10 seconds
6. Repeat 8-10 times

The motion is gentle. This is not a hard push — forceful bearing down raises abdominal pressure and works against you. Think "let go," not "force out."

### Step 3: Add Pelvic Floor Stretches

Positions that open the hips and pelvis give the muscles physical slack, making it easier to release them. A few that target the male pelvic floor:

- **Child's pose**: Kneel and sit back onto your heels, arms extended forward, and breathe into your lower back for 60-90 seconds
- **Happy baby**: Lie on your back, draw both knees toward your armpits, and hold the outsides of your feet while relaxing your pelvis for 60 seconds
- **Deep squat (malasana)**: Squat with heels down and knees wide, letting your pelvis sink between your thighs

Combine each stretch with slow diaphragmatic breathing. Our guide to [pelvic floor stretches](/blog/pelvic-floor-stretches) has the full sequence with positioning details.

## Building the Relaxation Habit

Relaxing a tight pelvic floor is a daily practice, not a one-time fix. You are unwinding a tension pattern that took months or years to build, so consistency matters more than intensity.

Aim for **5-10 minutes of relaxation work per day**. A simple structure: 5 minutes of diaphragmatic breathing, a round of reverse kegels, and one or two stretches. Attach it to an existing routine — before sleep, after a shower, or as a wind-down at the end of the day when tension is highest.

Awareness through the day matters too. Many men unconsciously clench the pelvic floor while sitting at a desk, driving, or under stress — the same way people clench their jaw. Check in a few times a day: is your pelvic floor gripping right now? If so, exhale and let it drop.

## Common Mistakes When Relaxing the Pelvic Floor

**Forcing the reverse kegel.** Bearing down hard is not relaxation — it is straining, and it increases downward pressure. The movement should feel like a gentle release, barely more than letting go.

**Doing regular kegels anyway.** If your pelvic floor is genuinely hypertonic, adding strengthening contractions can worsen pain and tension. Hold off on standard kegels until the tightness resolves, then reintroduce them gradually if needed. If you are unsure which category you fall into, our guide on [pelvic floor dysfunction](/blog/pelvic-floor-dysfunction) helps you tell the difference.

**Holding your breath.** Breath-holding locks the pelvic floor in a braced position. Keep breathing slowly and continuously through every exercise — the breath is doing half the work.

**Expecting overnight results.** A tension pattern this ingrained releases gradually. Give it several weeks of daily practice before judging progress.

## Common Questions

**Is a tight pelvic floor causing my premature ejaculation?**

It can contribute. An overactive pelvic floor disrupts the normal timing of the ejaculatory reflex, and some men find that learning to relax and control these muscles improves their staying power. [Healthline's overview of kegel exercises for men](https://www.healthline.com/health/kegel-exercises-for-men) is a useful plain-English primer, and our guide on [how to last longer in bed](/blog/how-to-last-longer-in-bed) covers the control side in depth.

**Should I see a professional?**

If you have significant pelvic pain, urinary symptoms, or pain with ejaculation, a [pelvic floor physical therapist](/blog/pelvic-floor-physical-therapy) can assess your muscle tone directly and give hands-on treatment. Self-directed relaxation work is a strong starting point, but persistent or severe symptoms deserve a proper evaluation.

**Can I relax and strengthen at the same time?**

Eventually, yes — a healthy pelvic floor both contracts and releases well. But if you are starting from a tight, painful baseline, lead with relaxation first. Once you can reliably let the muscles go, balanced training that includes both directions becomes appropriate.

**What if breathing alone does not help?**

Breathing is the foundation, but stubborn tension may also need stretching, manual therapy, or trigger-point work from a specialist. If a few weeks of consistent breathing and reverse kegels bring no change, escalate to a professional assessment.

## Start With the Exhale

Learning how to relax pelvic floor muscles is a genuine skill, and for men with a tight, overworked pelvic floor it matters far more than any squeeze. Begin with the breath, add reverse kegels, layer in stretches, and check your tension through the day. Give it consistent daily practice and the grip starts to loosen.

The direction to train is down and out — release, not effort.
