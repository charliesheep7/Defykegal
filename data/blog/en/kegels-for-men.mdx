## Why Kegels Aren't Just for Women

Kegels have a branding problem. Most men associate them with pregnancy and postpartum recovery and assume they have nothing to gain. That assumption costs them. The pelvic floor — the hammock of muscle running from the tailbone to the pubic bone — does the same core jobs in men as in women, plus a few that men care about a lot: it helps trap blood in the penis during an erection and controls the muscles that fire at climax.

Train that muscle and you're not chasing a vague wellness benefit. You're working the exact hardware behind erection firmness, ejaculatory control, and bladder control. **Kegels for men** are simply resistance training for a muscle group most guys have never deliberately worked in their lives.

## What the Pelvic Floor Actually Does

Picture a supportive sling stretched across the base of your pelvis. It holds up the bladder and bowel, wraps around the base of the penis, and squeezes shut the tubes that control urine flow. During an erection, two of these muscles — the ischiocavernosus and bulbocavernosus — clamp down to keep blood trapped in the erectile tissue. At climax, the same muscles contract rhythmically to power ejaculation.

That's why a weak or uncoordinated pelvic floor shows up as softer erections, less control over when you finish, and post-pee dribble. And it's why training the muscle can move all three at once. [Healthline](https://www.healthline.com/health/kegel-exercises-for-men) notes that men may benefit from kegels if they leak urine, dribble after urinating, or want to improve sexual function.

## What the Research Shows

This isn't fitness-influencer hype — the clinical record is solid.

For erections, the landmark study came from physiotherapist Grace Dorey and colleagues. In a randomized controlled trial, men with erectile dysfunction did pelvic floor exercises with biofeedback for six months. **Around 40 percent regained normal erectile function and another 35 percent improved**, versus no meaningful change in the control group ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16104916/)). That's a serious result for a drug-free intervention.

For ejaculatory control, a 2014 study by Pastore and colleagues in [*Therapeutic Advances in Urology*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4003840/) ran 40 men with lifelong premature ejaculation through 12 weeks of pelvic floor rehabilitation. **33 of 40 (82.5 percent) regained control**, with average latency rising from about 40 seconds to 146 seconds.

For bladder control, pelvic floor training is a first-line recommendation — [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22211-kegel-exercises-for-men) notes kegels help many men reduce urinary leaks and dribbling. The through-line: one muscle group, three payoffs.

## How to Do Kegels for Men

Getting the technique right matters more than doing a huge number of reps. Here's the Mayo Clinic-aligned method.

1. **Find the right muscles.** Imagine you're trying to stop the flow of urine midstream or hold in gas. The muscles that tighten are your pelvic floor. To confirm, you should feel a lift and squeeze around the anus — not a clench of your abs or glutes.
2. **Squeeze for 3 seconds, relax for 3.** Contract firmly, then release completely. The relaxation phase is not optional — a pelvic floor that can't relax is tense, not strong.
3. **Do 10 to 15 reps, three times a day.** That's the standard clinical dose. Spread the sets across the day.
4. **Isolate the muscle.** Keep your stomach, thighs, and buttocks relaxed, and don't hold your breath. Breathe normally through each rep.
5. **Add quick flicks as you progress.** Once slow holds feel easy, add fast contract-release pulses. These build the reflexive control you use during sex.

The single biggest mistake is only squeezing and never fully relaxing, which leaves the muscle chronically tight — a condition that can actually worsen pain and control. Train both directions. For a structured routine, see our [male pelvic floor workout](/blog/male-pelvic-floor-workout) guide.

## What Progress Looks Like

Give it time. [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22211-kegel-exercises-for-men) notes that with regular practice, results typically show up within a few weeks to a few months. Bladder control — fewer dribbles and leaks — often improves first because it's the most direct effect. Firmer erections and better ejaculatory control build over the longer window, matching the three-to-six-month timelines in the studies.

Consistency beats intensity. Ten good reps done daily will outperform fifty sloppy ones done twice a week. If your progress stalls, a pelvic floor physiotherapist can check your form and add biofeedback.

## Common Questions

**Can I do kegels anywhere?**
Yes. Once you can isolate the muscle, no one can tell you're doing them. Sitting at a desk, in the car, standing in line — they're invisible.

**How many kegels should a man do per day?**
The common clinical target is three sets of 10 to 15 reps daily. More isn't better; form and consistency are what matter.

**Can kegels make erections firmer?**
The pelvic floor muscles help trap blood in the penis, so strengthening them supports firmer erections — which is the mechanism behind the Dorey trial results. Our guide to [pelvic floor dysfunction in men](/blog/pelvic-floor-dysfunction) explains the connection in more depth, and you can reach out through the [Defy support page](/support) with questions.

**Is it possible to overdo kegels?**
Yes. Constantly clenching without relaxing can lead to a tight, overactive pelvic floor and pain. Follow the squeeze-and-fully-relax pattern rather than holding tension all day.

## The Bottom Line

Kegels for men aren't a niche or a novelty. They train a real muscle group with real jobs — erections, ejaculatory control, and bladder function — and the clinical evidence backs meaningful improvement across all three. The technique is simple: find the muscle, squeeze and fully relax, ten to fifteen reps, three times a day.

The catch is consistency. Do them right, every day, for a couple of months, and the payoff compounds.
