## The Muscle Most Men Never Train

You've trained your chest, your legs, maybe your core. But there's one muscle group doing critical work every day that almost no man has ever deliberately worked: the pelvic floor. **Pelvic floor exercises for men** target the sling of muscle at the base of your pelvis — the same muscles that keep you dry, help you stay firm, and control when you finish.

Here's the gap. Most guys who try these exercises get the technique subtly wrong from day one — clenching their abs, holding their breath, or bearing down instead of lifting — and then wonder why nothing changes. Get the form right and this is one of the highest-return, lowest-effort things you can do for your urinary and sexual health. This is how to do them properly.

## Why They Work

The pelvic floor isn't a vague wellness target. It's functional hardware. During an erection, two of these muscles — the ischiocavernosus and bulbocavernosus — clamp down to trap blood in the penis and keep it firm. At climax, the same muscles fire rhythmically to power ejaculation. And around the clock, they squeeze the urethra shut to hold urine in.

Train them and you're working the exact machinery behind erection firmness, ejaculatory control, and bladder control. The research backs this up hard. In a randomized controlled trial by physiotherapist Grace Dorey, men with erectile dysfunction who trained their pelvic floor for six months saw **about 40 percent regain normal function and another 35 percent improve** ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16104916/)). For post-surgery bladder control, a review found the majority of men doing pelvic floor exercises regained continence within six months — far more than those who didn't. This is resistance training for muscles that pay you back where it counts.

## Step-by-Step: How to Do Them Right

Technique beats volume every time. Follow these steps exactly.

1. **Find the right muscles.** Imagine you're trying to stop the flow of urine midstream *and* hold in gas at the same time. That inward-and-upward squeeze is your pelvic floor. You can confirm by actually stopping your urine stream once — but only once, as a test, never as a regular exercise (doing it habitually can mess with bladder emptying).

2. **Get into position.** Start lying down — it's the easiest way to isolate the muscle without gravity working against you. Once you're confident, you can do them sitting or standing anywhere.

3. **Squeeze and lift.** Contract the pelvic floor, lifting up and in. Hold for **3 seconds.** Focus on lifting, not bearing down.

4. **Relax fully.** Release for a full **3 seconds.** The relaxation is not a rest break — it's half the exercise. A muscle that only ever tightens becomes a problem of its own.

5. **Isolate.** Keep your stomach, thighs, and buttocks relaxed the whole time. If your abs are tensing, you're recruiting the wrong muscles. And keep breathing normally — never hold your breath.

6. **Do your sets.** Aim for **10 to 15 reps, three times a day.** As you get stronger over a few weeks, work up to holding each squeeze for up to 10 seconds.

That's the core routine used across [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22211-kegel-exercises-for-men) and [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/healthy-aging-and-longevity/pelvic-floor-exercises-help-for-incontinence-sexual-health-and-more) guidance. For a deeper breakdown of the movement, see our full guide to [kegels for men](/blog/kegels-for-men) and [pelvic floor muscles](/blog/pelvic-floor-muscles).

## Building the Habit

The exercises only work if you actually do them — and daily consistency is where most men fall off. The trick is to anchor the sets to things you already do every day: one set after you brush your teeth in the morning, one at a red light or your desk mid-afternoon, one before bed. Because nobody can see you doing them, you can train almost anywhere.

Progression matters too. Just like lifting weights, you build by gradually increasing hold time and reps — not by grinding out the same easy set forever. That's the piece that's hard to track in your head, and it's exactly where a structured program pays off.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even motivated men sabotage their progress with a few predictable errors:

- **Clenching the wrong muscles.** If your abs, glutes, or thighs are working, you're not isolating the pelvic floor. Put a hand on your belly — it should stay soft.
- **Holding your breath.** Bracing your breath recruits your core and defeats the isolation. Breathe normally throughout.
- **Bearing down instead of lifting.** Pushing *out* is the opposite of what you want and can strain the floor over time.
- **Skipping the relaxation.** Only squeezing, never fully releasing, can lead to a too-tight, [hypertonic pelvic floor](/blog/hypertonic-pelvic-floor) — which causes its own pain and dysfunction. If squeezing exercises ever make your symptoms worse, stop and get assessed.
- **Doing them only when you remember.** Sporadic effort gets sporadic results. Daily is the whole game.

## Common Questions

**Can I do too many?** Yes. Overtraining without full relaxation can leave the floor chronically tense. Stick to the recommended sets and prioritize clean reps over big numbers.

**Do I need any equipment?** No. Your own muscle contraction is the resistance. Some men add tools later, but they're optional.

**What if I can't feel the muscle at all?** That's common early on and usually improves within a couple of weeks as the mind–muscle connection develops. If you still can't locate it after a few weeks, a [pelvic floor physical therapist](/blog/pelvic-floor-physical-therapy) can help with biofeedback.

**Will this help premature ejaculation too?** Often, yes. The same muscles involved in erections play a role in ejaculatory control, and studies show pelvic floor training can improve it alongside erectile function and part of a broader [erectile dysfunction treatment](/blog/erectile-dysfunction-treatment) plan.

## The Bottom Line

Pelvic floor exercises for men are simple, private, and backed by real clinical evidence — but only when the technique is right. Lift, don't bear down. Relax as deliberately as you squeeze. Keep everything else loose. Do a few honest sets a day and give it a couple of months. Train the one muscle group you've been ignoring, and it pays you back in the areas men care about most.
